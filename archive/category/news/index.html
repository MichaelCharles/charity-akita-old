Goin’ Green in Akita

On Sunday, March 20th, the Akita Association of JETs held the annual St. Patrick’s Day Party at Studio London. With over 90 in attendance, including volunteers, the event drew a diverse crowd from all over Akita Prefecture.

Green was the theme of the night, with the venue covered in glowing clover and Irish adornment, including a Leprechaun Sugichi for that Akita touch.

Guest DJs turned it up and kept the dance floor occupied with trendy tunes, spinning tracks well on into the night.

This event raised over ¥66,000 which will be donated to the charity Blue For Tohoku, a non-profit organization that supports the communities effected by the aftermath of the March 11th, Great East Japan Earthquake.

Over all, it was a great night for meeting new people, hanging out with friends and celebrating Irish culture.

Akita Prefecture February Winter Festivals

Living out here in the boonies of Japan gives us access to a unique variety of cultural experiences. Particularly, every February, there’s a plethora of festivals available for you to enjoy. We’ve put together this short guide to give you a better idea about what’s out there and when things are happening. Keep in mind though, that these are just some of our favorites. There are many more festivals available during these winter months. 

Paper Balloon Festival

On February 10 in Nishiki.

The Paper Balloon festival, or Kamifusen-age, is a yearly event in Nishiki wherein paper lantern balloons carry the wishes of the community into the sky. Unfortunately, this festival happens on the same day as the Kariwano Tug-of-War, meaning you’ll only be able to attend one or the other.

Kariwano Tug-of-War

On February 10 in Daisen.

The Kariwano Tug-of-War is one of the most popular February festivals among ALTs. In this festival, the town is divided into two teams which play a giant game of tug-of-war, pulling a 10 ton, 200 meter long rope. If team one wins, people say that it will be a rich year. If team two wins, people say that the price of rice will rise.

Namahage Sedo Festival

On February 12-14 in Oga.

Another favorite of ALTs is the Namahage Sedo festival. This festival is one of the best ways to experience what the Namahage Museum calls Akita’s “intangible cultural heritage”. The mountain gods, Namahage, come down the mountain bearing torches to search for disobedient and lazy children and wives.

Akita Inukko Festival

On February 13-14 in Yuzawa.

Based in a tradition of crafting small statues of dogs, cranes, and tortoises and placing them in entrances of homes to ward off potential bandits, at the Akita Inukko Festival locals build giant statues of dogs in front of snow shrines. People see it as a way to bring protection from wrongdoing, as well as pray for a good harvest.

Candy Market Festival

On February 13-14 in Odate.

The Candy Market festival or Amekko-ichi has been held in Odate for centuries. The trees are decorated with pink candies, while venders sell sweets along O-machi. According to tradition, if you eat these sweets you will not catch a cold for the rest of the winter.

Fire Twirling Kamakura Festival

On February 13-14 in Kakunodate.

In the Kakunodate Fire Swinging Kamakura (Hiburi Kamakura) festival, participants light fire to small bales of rice straw, which they then twirl, by a string, around themselves. It is thought that the afterimage of the twirling fire looks like an igloo (kamakura in Japanese), thus it is called the Fire Twirling Kamakura festival.

Bamboo Battle Festival

On February 15 in Misato Town.

The Bamboo Battle festival or Takeuchi-matsuri is one of the most brutal festivals you will experience in Japan. The town is divided into north and south teams, who then battle it out using giant bamboo sticks surrounded by spectators in a battlefield lit by a giant bonfire.

Kamakura Festival

On February 15-16 in Yokote.

The Yokote Kamakura festival (Kamakura-matsuri) sees the town filled with differently styled kamakura (igloos) and snow sculptures. Inside the igloo are altars to oshizu, water gods, where sake and rice cakes are offered. People pray to the gods for clean water in the new year.

AAJ Financial Records

Hey, everyone. Today AAJ would like to announce something to you that we have been working on the past two months:* our newly organized, fully transparent collection of AAJ Financial Records. Here you can access a number of files that will give you a better idea about how we operate and raise money for charities within the prefecture and across the country. Read on for more details.

AAJ Event Financial Reports

This first group of documents is great if you are looking for just a quick snapshot of the financial details of any one event. In the “AAJ Event Financial Reports” folder you will find be able to find simple overviews of any AAJ event, including: date, location, attendance, revenue, expenditures, and charity/donation details.

We suspect that for most people these summaries present almost exactly what you’ll want to know about a given AAJ event, but we’ve got more information we can share with you if you are looking for more details.

Event Expenditures and Receipts

The second group of files can be found in the “Event Expenditures and Receipts” folder. This folder contains a separate folder for each AAJ event, which in turn contains a spreadsheet which is used to calculate the total cost of said event. Alongside the spreadsheet, you will be able to find scans of all of the receipts from that event, including those for supplies, the venue, transportation of equipment, and any other associated costs.

Bank Account

The third group of files that you might be interested in are those in the “Bank Account” folder. Here you can find digital scans of the AAJ bank book, showing you exactly what was in the account at any given time, along with details about what goes in and what comes out.

Along with these scans is an easier to view spreadsheet which lists the exact same information as the bank book, but with greater detail about each transaction.

Donation Receipts

Finally, you can take a look at the last folder, “Donation Receipts”, to confirm that all of the money raised from our events is actually making its way to the promised charities. It’s as simple as that: this folder is a repository for scans of all receipts received after we make our donations.

We hope these documents have helped you understand AAJ better and give you confidence that every yen we bring in is used exactly how was promised. All of these documents will be updated regularly (within a week of any transaction is our goal), and we will do our best to fill in information from before the new team took over if it is available. If you have any suggestions on how to improve our records, please feel free to pass them along. We’d also be happy to answer any questions you have about our finances at any time.

 

*This has been just about complete for a little more than a month now, but we wanted to wait until the Welcome Party money had been donated before putting up this post (the charity didn’t get their furikomi info to us until last week).
